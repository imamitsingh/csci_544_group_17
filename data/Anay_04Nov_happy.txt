Up to eight meters long and just a few centimetres in diameter, it is the principal traffic artery from the supermarket shelves to the bloodstream. From the start to the finish of our lives, innumerable tonnes of food and thousands of litres of fluid flow through it, which it digests with the help of numerous enzymes, making valuable nutrients available to us. But at the same time, countless pathogens and toxins also enter the gut and these have to be rendered harmless and evacuated.  Hard times for intestines 💚Unfortunately, the “interior design” of the gut has been turned upside down by modern eating and lifestyles. Its mucosa have been damaged by too much sugar and fat, low-fibre foods, and the shedloads of additives that are added to ready meals. It is estimated that around eight kilograms of preservatives pass through the gut during the life of an adult. And these compounds are anything but friendly to the intestinal flora! Since it is their job to destroy bacteria in food (to increase shelf life), they also destroy the bacteria in the gut. And they do not differentiate between gut bacteria that cause disease and those that help keep us healthy.  A lack of exercise, stress, pollution, medicines and many other factors also contribute to tipping the gut out of kilter. The gut is robust enough not be shaken by troublemakers that show up every now and again. Unfortunately, crowds of these disruptive elements are coming along ever more frequently and attacking the gut’s inhabitants, who are unable to resist such attacks indefinitely. And this can make itself felt in a variety of disorders, such as constipation, diarrhoea, bloating, IBS or inflammation of the gut.But that’s not all: a weakened gut is only able to absorb some of the valuable nutrients in food, as digestion, the breakdown of the food into its smallest components, is incomplete and insufficient. A gentle helping hand from nature 💚Natural swelling agents and dietary fibre, linseed, Indian psyllium, wheat and oat bran or pectin are ideal for regenerating the intestinal mucosa and colonising the gut with health-promoting intestinal bacteria.A multitude of vegetables, herbs and spices, such as garlic, wild garlic, radish, cress, onion, curcuma, ginger, basil, thyme and many others, are said to have antibiotic properties and help combat fungi, bacteria and viruses that often colonise the gut and are harmful for a healthy intestinal environment.A well-functioning gut is an asset whatever your diet and supports assimilation in the simplest way possible.Author: @Silvia Bürkle - @HQ Metabolic BalanceSilvia MischlerSilvia BürkleRainer Herbert KELLER#metabolicbalance #metabolism #happy #healthy #treatment #wellbeing#mikrobiom #probiotika #immunsystem
